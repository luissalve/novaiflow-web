## 2. What generative AI is

**In one sentence:** generative AI is a computer assistant that creates new text (and images, and more) by predicting what words usually come next, based on having "read" an enormous amount of writing.

That definition is accurate, but a mental model is more useful day to day:

> **Think of it as a fast, well-read colleague who has read almost everything — but who can be confidently wrong.**

This colleague:

- **Is fast.** It drafts an email, a summary, or a list in seconds.
- **Is well-read.** It can write in many styles, in English and Spanish, on almost any general topic.
- **Is eager to help.** It always gives you an answer.
- **Can be confidently wrong.** It sometimes states incorrect facts, invented names, or made-up numbers — and it says them with total confidence.

That last point is the most important thing in this whole manual. The AI is a brilliant *drafting* partner, not a source of truth. You bring the judgment; it brings the speed. We'll return to this in the golden rules.

## 3. How to talk to it

Modern AI assistants work like a **chat**: you type a message, the assistant replies, and you keep going in the same conversation. The assistant remembers what you said earlier in that conversation, so you can build on it.

The single most important habit is this:

> **Refine — don't restart.**

Beginners often type a request, get a so-so answer, and start a brand-new chat to try again. Don't. The faster path is to *stay in the conversation* and tell the assistant what to change. It already has the context; just steer it.

| Instead of starting over… | …say this in the same chat |
|---|---|
| The email is too long | "Make it about half as long." |
| The tone feels stiff | "Make it warmer and more friendly." |
| It missed the deadline | "Add that the deadline is Friday at 5 PM." |
| It's too formal for a text | "Rewrite this as a short, casual text message." |
| It's in the wrong language | "Translate that into Spanish." |

Think of it as a back-and-forth with a colleague. You wouldn't fire them and hire a new one because the first draft wasn't perfect — you'd give feedback. Do the same here.

## 4. The anatomy of a good prompt


*Four simple parts — Role · Task · Context · Format — turn a vague request into a clear one.*

A "prompt" is simply the instruction you give the AI. A vague prompt gets a vague answer. A clear prompt gets a useful answer. The easiest way to be clear is to include four things: **Role · Task · Context · Format.**

| Part | Question it answers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Role** | Who should the AI act as? | "You are a friendly customer-service rep." |
| **Task** | What exactly do you want done? | "Write a reply to this complaint." |
| **Context** | What does it need to know? | "The customer's package arrived 3 days late." |
| **Format** | How should the answer look? | "Keep it under 100 words, polite, no excuses." |

You don't always need all four — but the more of them you include, the better the result. Here is a fill-in template you can keep next to your keyboard:

```
Role:    You are a {kind of professional} who is {helpful trait}.
Task:    {What you want done, in one clear sentence.}
Context: {The facts the AI needs: who, what, when, key details.}
Format:  {Length, tone, language, structure — e.g. "a short email in Spanish".}
```

### Worked example 1 — a client email (logistics)

```
Role:    You are a logistics coordinator who communicates clearly and calmly.
Task:    Write an email letting a client know their shipment is delayed.
Context: Shipment #4471 was due Tuesday; it will now arrive Thursday because
         of a port inspection. The client is {Maria Gonzalez}. We are not
         charging extra.
Format:  A short, professional email in English, under 120 words, reassuring
         tone, ending with our contact line.
```

### Worked example 2 — a meeting summary (any office)

```
Role:    You are an executive assistant who writes tidy, scannable notes.
Task:    Turn my rough meeting notes below into a clean summary.
Context: {Paste your messy notes here.}
Format:  A summary with three sections — Decisions, Action Items (with owners),
         and Open Questions. Use bullet points.
```

Notice how each prompt reads like instructions to a capable assistant. That's exactly the right mindset.

## 5. Everyday use cases

Here are the tasks people reach for most often. Each one comes with a copy-paste prompt — replace the `{curly braces}` with your own details.

**1. Write or polish an email**

```
Write a polite, professional email to {recipient} about {topic}.
Keep it under {120} words. Tone: {warm but businesslike}. End with a clear
next step.
```

**2. Summarize a long document or thread**

```
Summarize the text below for a busy manager. Give me the 5 most important
points as bullets, then one line on what action is needed.

{Paste the long text here.}
```

**3. Translate between English and Spanish**

```
Translate the message below into {Spanish}. Keep the meaning and a {friendly,
professional} tone. Do not translate proper names or product codes.

{Paste the message here.}
```

**4. Brainstorm ideas**

```
Give me {10} ideas for {a social post promoting our weekend sale}. Make them
short, varied, and easy to act on. Aim them at {local families}.
```

**5. Build a quick table**

```
Turn the information below into a clean table with columns for
{Name, Date, Status, Next Step}.

{Paste or list the information here.}
```

**6. "Explain it simply"**

```
Explain {what an SNMP printer alert means} in plain language, as if to a
new employee. Use a short example and avoid jargon.
```

Keep this section bookmarked. These six prompts alone cover a surprising share of daily office work.

## 6. Refine & iterate

The first answer is a starting point, not the finish line. Watch how two quick refinements turn an average draft into exactly what you need.

**Your first prompt:**

```
Write an email to a tenant reminding them rent is due.
```

The AI gives you a correct but generic email. Now refine — in the same chat:

**Refinement 1 (add context and tone):**

```
Make it friendlier, and mention that rent is due on the 1st and there's a
$50 late fee after the 5th. Sign it from "The Sunrise Property Team."
```

**Refinement 2 (adjust format):**

```
Perfect — now make it shorter, and give me a Spanish version below the
English one.
```

In three short steps you went from a blank page to a polished, bilingual, on-brand reminder. That's the whole skill: **start simple, then steer.** You rarely need the "perfect" first prompt — you need the willingness to give one or two pieces of feedback.

## 7. The three golden rules of responsible use

AI is powerful, and that power comes with responsibility. These three rules keep you, your clients, and your company safe. They apply at every level of this program.

### Rule 1 — Protect sensitive data

**Never paste sensitive information into a public AI tool without an approved company policy.** That includes patient health details (PHI), personal data like Social Security or account numbers (PII), client confidential files, and anything covered by privilege or contract.

A simple test: *if you wouldn't post it publicly or email it to a stranger, don't paste it into a public AI tool.* When in doubt, remove names and numbers first, or ask your manager which tool is approved for sensitive work.

### Rule 2 — Keep a human in the loop

**AI drafts; a person reviews and owns the result.** You are always the editor and the author of record. Before anything goes to a client, a patient, a court, or the public, a qualified person reads it, corrects it, and takes responsibility for it. The AI never has the final word — you do.

### Rule 3 — Verify facts and figures

**Treat every fact, name, number, date, law, and quote as "needs checking."** Remember the well-read colleague who can be confidently wrong. AI sometimes invents details that look perfectly real — this is called a *hallucination*. For anything that matters, confirm it against a trusted source before you use it.

> Three more habits round these out across the program: **disclose** AI use where your industry or client requires it, **respect confidentiality**, and follow your **company standard** (shared templates and criteria) instead of improvising on your own.

## 9. Quick FAQ

**Do I need to code?**
No. Nothing in this program requires programming. You type in plain English or Spanish, exactly as you'd write to a colleague.

**Which tool should I use?**
For this level, any reputable chat-based AI assistant works. Your instructor will point you to the specific tool used in class. The skills you learn transfer between tools — the Role · Task · Context · Format pattern works everywhere.

**Is my data safe?**
Treat public AI tools like a public space. Don't paste sensitive or confidential information unless your company has an approved policy and tool for it (see Golden Rule 1). For everyday, non-sensitive tasks, you're fine. When in doubt, ask.

**Will it replace my job?**
No. It's a tool that removes busywork so you can focus on the parts only a person can do — judgment, relationships, and responsibility. The human stays in charge.

**What if it gives me a wrong answer?**
Expect it occasionally. That's why you verify (Golden Rule 3) and why a person always reviews before anything goes out (Golden Rule 2).
