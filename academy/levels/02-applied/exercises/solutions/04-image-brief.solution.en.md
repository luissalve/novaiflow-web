# Solution — Exercise 4: Write an On-Brand Image Brief

This is a **model answer**, not the only right answer. A strong image brief (1) covers all six parts
(subject, style, colors, layout, text, mood/use), (2) names brand red `#E5002B` as the accent, and (3)
stays rights-safe with no real faces or copyrighted material. Use this to check your card.

> **For facilitators:** the two habits that matter most here are proofreading on-image text and
> confirming usage rights. A beautiful image with a misspelled word or a borrowed logo is unusable.

---

## A model brief (logistics social banner)

```text
Create an image for a LinkedIn post.
Subject: a stylized delivery truck driving along a sunny coastal highway, palm trees in the distance.
Style: clean modern flat illustration, simple shapes, no photorealism.
Colors: mostly white and light gray; use brand red #E5002B as the accent (the truck cab and a thin underline).
Layout: horizontal banner (16:9); the truck on the right third, open white space on the left for a headline.
Text in image: the text "Fast Freight, Done Right" — keep it short and spelled exactly this way, top-left.
Mood: confident, professional, friendly.
Avoid: real people's faces, any company logos, clutter, busy backgrounds.
```

### What a good result looks like

A simple, uncluttered horizontal banner: clean white space on the left holding the headline, a flat
illustrated truck on the right with the cab in brand red, a calm coastal backdrop. It reads in one
glance and clearly belongs to the brand.

---

## Why this works — part by part

| Part | In the model | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Subject** | "stylized delivery truck… coastal highway" | Concrete scene, not "something about shipping." |
| **Style** | "clean modern flat illustration, no photorealism" | Sets a consistent, repeatable look. |
| **Colors** | "brand red `#E5002B` as the accent" | On-brand by design, not by luck. |
| **Layout** | "truck right, open space left for headline" | Leaves room for text; composition is intentional. |
| **Text** | exact short phrase, spelled out | Limits what the AI can misspell. |
| **Avoid list** | "no faces, no logos, no clutter" | Heads off the most common rights and quality problems. |

---

## The review step (where images go wrong)

| Check | What to confirm |
|---|---|
| **Spelling** | Read on-image text **letter by letter** — AI often garbles words. |
| **Brand color** | Is the red the actual accent, or did it flood the background? |
| **Rights** | No real person's face, no trademarked logo, no copyrighted character. |
| **Accuracy** | If it implies a claim (e.g. "fastest"), is that claim true and allowed? |
| **Fit** | Right orientation and space for where it'll actually be posted? |

If anything fails, refine with a small instruction ("fix the text to read exactly 'Fast Freight, Done
Right'", "use red only on the truck") rather than starting over.

---

## Common mistakes and quick fixes

| Mistake | Why it hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Make a nice shipping image" | Generic, off-brand result | Fill all six parts of the brief |
| Said "red" but not `#E5002B` | Wrong shade of red | Always name the exact brand hex |
| Long paragraph of on-image text | AI misspells it | Keep on-image text to a few words and proofread |
| Asked for a real logo or celebrity | Rights violation | Keep it generic and rights-safe |
| Published without review | Typos or claims slip through | A person reviews and owns every image |

---

## Rationale (the one big idea)

An image brief is a prompt with a **visual checklist**: subject, style, colors, layout, text, mood. Name
all six (and your exact brand red) and the result lands on-brand the first time. But images carry two
extra risks text doesn't — **misspelled words** and **usage rights** — so the human review step is
non-negotiable: proofread the letters, confirm you can legally use it, then publish.
