# Level 2 · Knowledge Check (Quiz)

A short, friendly check to confirm the key ideas from Applied landed. There are **10 questions** — a
mix of multiple-choice and short-answer. It is **optional** and small (worth about 10% of your level
score); your **team template kit** is what really earns the certificate. Take about 12 minutes. You
may answer in English or Spanish.

> No tricks here. If a question makes you pause, that is a good sign — it means you are thinking like a
> careful AI user. The **answer key** is at the bottom.

---

## Instructions

1. Answer all 10 questions. For multiple-choice, write the letter. For short-answer, one or two
   sentences is plenty.
2. Use your own words — there is no need to memorize definitions.
3. When you finish, check yourself against the answer key. Anything you miss is just a quick review.

---

## Part A — Multiple choice (1–6)

**1. What is a "template" in this level?**
- A. A finished email you copy and send without changes
- B. A reusable prompt with the changing parts marked as `{variables}`, so anyone can reuse it
- C. A picture of a form you fill out by hand
- D. A setting that makes the AI answer faster

**2. What makes a `{variable}` (placeholder) useful in a template?**
- A. It hides the prompt from your teammates
- B. It marks the parts that change each time, so the rest of the prompt stays locked and reusable
- C. It tells the AI to translate everything into Spanish
- D. It is required by law for all prompts

**3. You turn a long email thread into a short summary. What is the most important thing to check
before you trust it?**
- A. That the AI used bullet points
- B. That the summary kept the important details and changed no facts, figures, names or dates
- C. That it is exactly five sentences long
- D. That it sounds confident

**4. When does it make sense to switch to a "more capable" model or mode?**
- A. For every single message, no matter how small
- B. Never — the fastest model is always best
- C. For longer, data-heavy, or higher-stakes tasks where quality matters more than speed
- D. Only when the AI asks you to

**5. You ask the AI to build a table or a spreadsheet formula. What is the right way to use the result?**
- A. Trust the numbers and paste them straight into the report
- B. The AI describes the table or formula; a person rebuilds and checks it in the real spreadsheet
- C. Email the result to a coworker so they can decide
- D. Skip the table and write a paragraph instead

**6. Which image prompt is most likely to give you what you want?**
- A. "Make a nice image."
- B. "An image for our company."
- C. "A horizontal banner of a delivery truck at sunrise, flat modern style, brand red `#E5002B` accents, space for the text 'Same-day delivery'."
- D. "Something with a truck, you choose the rest."

## Part B — Short answer (7–10)

**7.** In one sentence, explain the difference between a **one-off prompt** and a **reusable template**.

**8.** Take this working prompt and turn it into a **template** by marking the parts that change as
`{variables}`:
> "Write a friendly reply to Maria López telling her that her order #4471 shipped today and arrives Friday."
(Mark at least three variables.)

**9.** Every template card in your kit must end with a **check-before-sending** line. In one sentence,
explain *why* — and give one example of something a person should verify.

**10.** A teammate built an image for a campaign and wants to publish it right away. Name **two**
things they should check before it goes out, and why.

---

## Answer key

> Self-check guide. Short-answer questions accept any reasonable wording — the ideas matter, not the
> exact words.

| # | Answer | Note |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **B** | A template is a reusable prompt with `{variables}` for the parts that change. |
| 2 | **B** | Variables mark what changes; the locked frame is what makes a template reliable. |
| 3 | **B** | Summaries can drop nuance or alter facts — verify the important parts. |
| 4 | **C** | Longer, data-heavy, higher-stakes work is where a more capable mode pays off. |
| 5 | **B** | AI describes the table/formula; a person rebuilds and verifies it in the real sheet. |
| 6 | **C** | Specific subject, style, colors, orientation and text get a usable image. |

**7.** A *one-off prompt* is written for one specific case and thrown away; a *reusable template* has
the changing parts marked as `{variables}` and a locked frame (role, tone, format), so a teammate can
reuse it the same way every time. *Acceptable if the learner captures "single use" vs. "reusable with
variables."*

**8.** A strong answer marks the changing details as variables and keeps the rest fixed. Example:
```text
Write a {tone} reply to {customer name} telling them that their order {order number}
shipped {ship day} and arrives {arrival day}.
```
*Full credit for at least three sensible variables (e.g., customer name, order number, ship day,
arrival day, tone) with the fixed frame kept intact.*

**9.** Because **AI drafts, but a person reviews and owns the output** (human-in-the-loop) — the
check-before-sending line names what to verify so nothing wrong goes out. *Examples of what to verify:*
a number or figure, a date, a name, a price, a policy, reschedule times, that no sensitive data slipped
in. *Full credit for "a person must review/own it" + one concrete verification.*

**10.** Any two of these (with a reason):
1. **Accuracy** — does the image actually show what was asked, with no wrong or misleading details?
2. **Rights / licensing** — are we allowed to use this image (no copyright or likeness problems)?
3. **Brand fit** — correct colors (brand red `#E5002B`), tone, and any on-image text spelled right?
4. **No sensitive data** — no real names, faces, or confidential info shown.
*Full credit for any two with a sensible "why" — the theme is "a person reviews before publishing."*
