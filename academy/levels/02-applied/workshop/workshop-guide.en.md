# Level 2 Workshop Guide — Build Your Team's Reusable Templates (48 minutes)

This is the facilitator and participant guide for the **48-minute guided workshop** in Level 2 ·
Applied. By the end, each small group leaves with **at least two finished, tested template cards** —
the same cards that become the seed of the **team template kit** they assemble in the project that
follows.

The workshop is the heart of the class (40% of the two hours). It comes right after the live demo,
where the group already watched a one-off prompt turn into a reusable template across writing,
summaries, spreadsheets and images. Here, participants do it themselves with real tasks from their
own week — no code, all in plain language, English or Spanish.

> **In one line:** stop rewriting prompts from scratch. Build a few **reusable molds** your whole
> team can pick up and use the same way, every time.

## Materials checklist

Before the workshop block starts, confirm you have:

- [ ] **Approved chat assistant open** on the facilitator screen (the same one used in the demo).
- [ ] **A blank shared document** open — the future template kit (a doc, wiki page, or shared note).
- [ ] **One finished template card** visible as the "target" everyone is building toward (use the example below).
- [ ] **Participant worksheet** printed or shared digitally (one per person — see the end of this guide).
- [ ] **The template card structure visible** at all times — on a slide, whiteboard, or printed card.
- [ ] **A device per participant** (laptop, tablet, or phone) signed in to the approved tool.
- [ ] **The prompt cheat-sheet** from `handouts/` within reach (it has ready Level 2 templates).
- [ ] **A "do-not-paste" reminder** visible: no PHI, PII, or confidential data in public tools (see Responsible AI below).
- [ ] **A timer** (phone or on-screen) so the six segments stay on track.
- [ ] **Two pre-built example templates** ready in case a group is stuck (one generic email, one for the room's main industry).

> **Bilingual note:** keep the template card structure and the worksheet visible in English *and*
> Spanish. If the room is mixed, say each instruction once in each language, or pair a bilingual
> participant with anyone who needs it. The assistant works equally well in either language — make
> that point early so Spanish-first participants build their templates in Spanish.

## How groups are organized

This workshop is built around **groups of 2–3 around one shared screen and one shared doc** — not
solo work. A template is a *team* asset, so building it together is the point.

1. Form groups of 2–3. Mixed roles are fine; mixed comfort levels are good (pair a confident user
   with a newer one).
2. Each group picks a **function** they all touch — front desk, dispatch, billing, sales, intake.
3. One person drives the keyboard; the others watch the output and call out improvements. Rotate
   the driver after the first template.

## Minute-by-minute facilitator script

The 48 minutes split into six segments. Keep the timer visible and call each transition out loud.

### 0–5 min · Frame the task (5 min)

**Goal:** everyone understands they are building **molds**, not writing one-off prompts.

1. Say it plainly: *"We're not writing prompts today — we're building templates. A template is a
   prompt with the changing parts marked as `{variables}`, so anyone can reuse it without starting over."*
2. Show **one finished template card** on screen (the target). Read it top to bottom.
3. Point at the `{variables}`: *"Everything in curly braces changes each time. Everything else stays
   locked — that's what makes it reliable."*
4. State the one rule for the room: *"We never paste anything private — no patient data, no client
   confidential info, no IDs. We practice with made-up sample data."*

```text
Name:                 Carrier delay reply
For which task:       Reply to a customer asking why their shipment is late
Template:             Act as a friendly bilingual customer-service rep for a freight company.
                      Draft a reply to {customer} whose shipment is delayed.
                      Context: order {order number}, due {original date}, new ETA {new date},
                      reason {reason}. In {language}, under {length} words, warm and professional,
                      ending with one clear next step.
When to use it:       A customer asks why their shipment is late
Check before sending: Order number, dates and reason are correct; a person reviews before sending
Language(s):          EN / ES
Owner:                Dispatch lead
```

**Facilitator tip:** keep this to five minutes. The value is in the building, not the framing.

### 5–11 min · Pick repeated tasks (6 min)

**Goal:** each group lists 3–5 tasks they repeat every week, then picks the best two to template.

1. *"List the tasks your group does over and over — emails you send, summaries you make, tables you
   build, simple images you need. Frequent and boring is exactly what we want."*
2. Have each group write 3–5 on their worksheet.
3. Coach the choice: *"Pick your top two. Favor tasks that are **frequent and low-risk** first —
   something safe to practice with today."*
4. Nudge variety: *"If you can, pick two **different types** — say one email and one summary or
   table — so your kit covers more ground."*

**Facilitator tip:** circulate and veto anything risky for a first template (anything that needs
real private data). Steer them to a safe version.

### 11–23 min · Build template #1 (12 min)

**Goal:** each group turns a real task into a working template with marked `{variables}`, tested once.

Walk the room and coach. Use this internal pacing:

| Sub-step | ~Time | What participants do | What you say / do |
|---|---|---|---|
| Write the base prompt | 11:00–14:00 | Use Role · Task · Context · Format on the chosen task. | "Start from the Level 1 formula — you already know this part." |
| Mark the variables | 14:00–17:00 | Underline what changes each time → wrap it in `{curly braces}`. | "What changes every time you do this? That's a variable." |
| Test on a real case | 17:00–20:00 | Fill the variables with **non-sensitive** sample data; run it. | Watch for private data; offer sample-data swaps. |
| Read & refine once | 20:00–23:00 | Read the output; improve with one instruction. | "Refine, don't redo. Add one instruction — 'shorter', 'warmer', 'add a next step'." |

**Coaching moves while you circulate:**

- Ask the magic question to surface variables: *"What changes each time you run this?"*
- If the output swings wildly between runs, tell them to **lock tone, format and length** in the template.
- Celebrate the first well-marked template out loud: *"Nice — this group turned 'the date' into `{date}` so it works every week."*
- Announce "wrap up template #1 in two minutes" at the 21-minute mark.

### 23–35 min · Build template #2 (12 min)

**Goal:** repeat the build for a **different task type**, so the group's kit isn't all emails.

1. *"Same steps, new task — and try a different type this time: a summary, a table, or a simple image."*
2. Rotate the keyboard driver so a second person practices.
3. Coach the type-specific watch-outs as you pass each group:
   - **Summary:** *"Check it didn't drop the important detail or change a number."*
   - **Spreadsheet/table:** *"The AI describes the table or formula — a person rebuilds and checks it in the real sheet."*
   - **Image:** *"Review for accuracy, rights, and brand fit before anyone publishes it. Brand red is `#E5002B`."*
4. Same discipline: mark `{variables}`, test once, refine once.

**Facilitator tip:** if a group is flying, challenge them to start a third template. If a group is
behind, it is fine to leave with one strong template plus a half-built second.

### 35–43 min · Test on a second case (8 min)

**Goal:** prove each template by running it on a **second, different** real case. This is the rule
that separates a template from a one-off prompt.

1. *"Now the real test: run each template again with **different** sample data. A template you only
   tried once is just a prompt that worked once."*
2. Have groups swap in new values for every `{variable}` and run both templates again.
3. If the second run needs heavy edits, the template isn't locked enough — coach them to add the
   missing constraint (tone, length, format) rather than rewrite from scratch.
4. *"Refine, don't redo. If it almost worked, fix the one thing — don't start over."*

**Facilitator tip:** make "no second test, no kit" a hard rule. It is the single most important
quality habit this level teaches.

### 43–48 min · Write the cards + save to the kit (5 min)

**Goal:** every working template becomes a finished **card** in the shared kit document.

1. Have each group fill the full card for each template: **Name · For which task · Template · When to
   use it · Check before sending · Language(s) · Owner.**
2. Each card **must** end with a check-before-sending line — that's the human-in-the-loop step.
3. Paste each finished card into the **shared kit document**.
4. Quick peer test: another group reads one card and confirms they could use it **with no extra
   explanation**. If not, fix the unclear part.
5. Bridge to the project: *"In the next 12 minutes we gather every group's cards into one kit and
   name an owner. That kit is your Level 2 deliverable — and the foundation for Level 3 automation."*

**Facilitator tip:** if the room is shy about peer-testing, do one yourself on screen with a card you
spotted while circulating (with the group's permission).

## What good looks like

A successful Level 2 template is **reusable, tested, and safe to share** — not a one-off prompt with
the details hard-coded.

| Quality | One-off prompt (not yet) | Reusable template (good) |
|---|---|---|
| Variables | Real names, dates, numbers typed in | Marked as `{variables}` so it works next week |
| Fixed frame | Role/tone/format change each run | Role, tone, language and format are **locked** |
| Tested | Tried once, on today's case | Tested on **two** different real cases |
| Card | Lives in one person's chat history | A **card** in the shared kit anyone can find |
| Review step | Output sent as-is | Ends with a **check-before-sending** line |
| Safe | Private data baked in | Sample/placeholder data only |

A great Level 2 template card looks like this:

```text
Name:                 No-show follow-up
For which task:       Message a patient who missed a routine appointment
Template:             Act as a warm bilingual front-desk coordinator for a dental clinic.
                      Draft a no-show follow-up message to {patient first name}.
                      Context: missed a {appointment type}; offer two reschedule options:
                      {option 1}, {option 2}. In {language}, under {length} words, friendly and
                      respectful, ready to send by text. No medical advice.
When to use it:       A patient misses a routine appointment
Check before sending: Reschedule times are real and open; a person reviews; no medical advice added
Language(s):          EN / ES
Owner:                Front-desk lead
```

## Common pitfalls and how to coach them

| Pitfall | What you'll see | How to coach it |
|---|---|---|
| **Template is really one specific prompt** | Real names, dates, numbers typed straight in. | "What changes each time? Wrap those parts in `{curly braces}`." |
| **Output varies wildly between runs** | Tone/length/format different every time. | "Lock the frame — state the tone, length and format inside the template." |
| **Skips the second test** | Group declares victory after one run. | Make it a rule: "No second test, no card. One run proves nothing." |
| **Pasting private data** | Real patient/client info, IDs. | Stop gently; swap in sample data; restate the policy. |
| **No review step on the card** | Card ends at the template text. | "Every card ends with check-before-sending. A person owns the output." |
| **Over-templating** | Trying to make one mega-template do everything. | "One template, one task. Keep each card self-contained." |
| **Rabbit-holing on one card** | Group polishes card #1 for 20 minutes. | "Good enough beats perfect. Save it, build the next one." |
| **Believing facts blindly** | A figure or policy stated as fact in the output. | "AI can be confidently wrong. Flag it on the card to verify before sending." |
| **All one type** | Five email templates, nothing else. | "Pick a different type — a summary, a table, an image — so the kit covers more." |

## Accessibility & bilingual tips

- **Say it, show it, hand it.** Every instruction goes out three ways: spoken, on screen, and on the
  printed worksheet — so no one depends on hearing alone.
- **Run the room in both languages.** Give each instruction once in English and once in Spanish, or
  let groups choose; encourage Spanish-first groups to build their templates in Spanish.
- **Large, high-contrast text** for the card structure and worksheet. Brand red `#E5002B` is for
  accents only — never the only way to convey meaning (pair it with a label).
- **Pace for everyone.** Offer "type or dictate" — the assistant accepts either. Rotate the keyboard
  driver so slower typers still participate.
- **No jargon left undefined.** If you must use a term (e.g., "variable"), define it once: *a variable
  is just the blank you fill in each time, written in `{curly braces}`.*
- **Group work is the default.** Two or three people sharing one screen and one template is the
  intended outcome, not a fallback.

## Responsible AI in this workshop (reinforce, don't lecture)

Weave these in naturally as you coach — one line each, when the moment is right:

1. **Never paste sensitive data** (PHI, PII, confidential) into public tools without an approved
   policy. *Today we practice with made-up sample data, and templates use `{variables}` instead of
   real details — which is exactly what makes them safe to share.*
2. **Human-in-the-loop:** every template card ends with a **check-before-sending** line. The AI
   drafts; a person reviews and owns what goes out.
3. **Verify facts & figures:** flag on the card anything that must be checked (a number, a date, a
   policy) before it is sent.
4. **Disclose where required** and respect confidentiality and privilege.
5. **Company standard over improvisation:** the kit you start here *is* your team's standard — the
   move from "everyone improvises" to "we all use the same proven molds."
